Overview

I had the opportunity to attend the annual Pacific Northwest Multifamily Forum by Marcus & Millichap, which focused on the current state of the Portland and Seattle apartment markets, key economic trends shaping the region, and what to expect moving forward. This report I put together provides an overview of the most important data, insights, and takeaways from the event highlighting how shifting market dynamics are creating new opportunities for owners and investors across the Pacific Northwest.

Executive Summary

After navigating several years of economic challenges, the Pacific Northwest multifamily market has reached a pivotal turning point. Nationwide, apartment fundamentals have stabilized as strong demand now exceeds a sharply declining construction pipeline, setting the stage for renewed rent growth and occupancy gains through 2027. Both Seattle and Portland are positioned to benefit from these improving conditions, though their recoveries follow different paths: Seattle has effectively absorbed its recent surge in new supply and is showing balanced performance, while Portland is experiencing a rapid rebound in downtown & in local submarkets. Persistent affordability pressures continue to keep renters in place, supporting steady occupancy and cash flow across existing assets. With limited future competition and improving market fundamentals, the outlook for property performance and long-term value appreciation remains highly favorable. The next few years may also present compelling acquisition opportunities for investors seeking to expand portfolios ahead of the next growth cycle.

Macroeconomic Environment: The National Backdrop

Economic Uncertainty and Tariff Volatility

  • Economic policy uncertainty peaked at a record high of 725 in April 2025; while it has eased to 317, it remains nearly double normal levels.
  • Previous spikes were tied to major global events like the Global Financial Crisis (2008), Brexit, and the Pandemic (2020).
  • The current effective tariff rate on U.S. imports is 18%, the highest since 1934 and a stark increase from the 2.4% historical average.

Summary: Elevated uncertainty and tariff pressures increase operational risks but also reinforce the value of apartments as a stable, inflation-hedging asset class. These conditions create headwinds for new development, benefiting existing properties.

A Cooling, Balanced Labor Market

  • Job growth decreased to August 2025, with large downward revisions to prior data, confirming a significant economic slowdown.
  • Annual job growth is forecast to slow from 2.6M in 2023 to just 900K in 2025.
  • The ratio of job openings to unemployed persons is now 1:1, shifting the market from overheated to "in balance."

Summary: The labor market slowdown is the primary catalyst for expected Federal Reserve rate cuts. These cuts will lower borrowing costs for acquisitions and refinancing, providing a direct financial benefit to property investors.

Inflation Cools, Paving Way for Rate Cuts

  • CPI inflation has fallen dramatically to 2.9% from a 9.1% peak, remaining just above the Fed's 2% target.
  • Businesses are currently absorbing the majority of tariff costs, but future pass-through to consumers poses an inflation risk.
  • The market is pricing in aggressive Fed easing, with a 94% probability of the Fed funds rate dropping to 3.50% by December.

Summary: Anticipated rate cuts create a favorable window for refinancing existing debt or acquiring new assets at a lower cost of capital. This monetary easing directly supports higher property values & more transactions looking forward.

National Outlook: The Bull Case for Existing Apartments

  • Demand Exceeds Supply: National net absorption (159K units) has surpassed new completions (119K units), flipping the market dynamic.
  • Vacancy Peaked: The national vacancy rate is declining from its recent 5.8% increase in 2023, signaling a landlord-favorable environment ahead.
  • Construction Collapsed: New apartment starts are down a staggering 76%, and under construction down 53% ensuring minimal new competition for the next 2-4 years.
  • Unaffordable Homeownership: Buying a home costs 66% more per month than renting, creating a larger audience of renters and sustaining robust apartment demand.

Summary: The combination of resurgent demand and a frozen construction pipeline creates a golden era for existing assets, supporting strong occupancy and above-average rent growth through 2027.

Portland Market Deep Dive

Portland's Economic & Housing Fundamentals

  • Portland's job growth has been essentially flat in 2025, with an unemployment rate of 4.3% matching the U.S. average.
  • A significant affordability gap exists, with the median Portland home payment ($2,399) far exceeding average rent.
  • The apartment market is healthy and balanced, with demand (1.5K units absorbed) slightly exceeding new supply (1.3K units completed).

Summary: The housing affordability crisis provides a strong backstop for rental demand, even with a flat job market. A balanced supply/demand dynamic creates a stable foundation for steady occupancy and rent growth.

A Tale of Two Markets: Downtown Soars, Suburbs Lag

  • Construction Evaporated: Units under construction have collapsed by a staggering 81% (from 13K to 2.4K), setting up a severely supply-constrained market for 2026-2027.
  • Vacancy Highest in Class C: Luxury Class A has the highest vacancy (5.8%), while Affordable Class C is tightest (4.1%), indicating more demand for class A & B apartments.
  • Dramatic Rent Bifurcation: Downtown (CBD) rents are seeing growth, while suburban rents are nearly flat.
  • Top Submarkets: Central Portland leads with rent growth, while suburban tech corridors like Hillsboro are struggling with a 4.9% decline.

Summary: A clear "flight to quality" is underway. Investment focus should be on urban core assets where demand is surging. The 81% drop in construction is the single most bullish indicator for future rent growth across the entire metro, creating significant upside for existing properties.

Secondary Markets Find Equilibrium

  • Boise: The boom/bust cycle has stabilized. Supply and demand are perfectly balanced (2.4K each), and rent growth is recovering at 3.3%.
  • Spokane: The market has reached equilibrium after rapid expansion. Vacancy is healthy at 4.8% and rent growth is flat but stable.
  • Tri-Cities: A small but balanced market with a 4.9% vacancy rate and steady 2.1% rent growth.

Summary: Key secondary markets in the region have successfully absorbed their construction waves and are now stable. This reduces regional volatility and points to a period of modest, steady growth, making them viable alternative investment markets.

Portland Market: Actionable Conclusion

The Portland multifamily market is defined by a high-velocity urban recovery and a powerful, supply-driven forward outlook. While suburban submarkets lag, the urban core's exceptional rent growth signals a prime investment opportunity. The virtual evaporation of the construction pipeline is the critical factor, ensuring minimal new competition and creating significant pricing power for existing assets into 2027. This translates directly to higher future rental income and supports property value appreciation. For investors and buyers, today’s environment presents a unique window to acquire quality assets before rent growth accelerates and pricing power compounds positioning acquisitions made now to outperform over the next 24–36 months.

Seattle Market Deep Dive

Seattle's Labor Headwinds & Housing Fundamentals

  • Seattle is underperforming the nation with a loss of 11,000 jobs year-to-date, driven by tech sector weakness.
  • The region has the most severe affordability crisis, with the median Seattle home payment ($3,173) creating a massive barrier to homeownership.
  • Despite job losses, apartment demand (6.9K units absorbed) is still slightly outpacing new supply (6.2K completions).

Summary: The extreme cost of homeownership provides a powerful, inelastic demand base for apartments that counteracts the soft job market. This dynamic protects property cash flows and supports stable occupancy levels even during economic weakness.

Seattle Market Reaches Healthy Equilibrium

  • Supply Glut Absorbed: Seattle has successfully worked through its oversupply, with vacancy stabilizing at a very healthy 4.0% but under construction down 60%.
  • Balanced Across the Board: Vacancy is remarkably consistent across locations (CBD vs. Suburban) and property classes (A, B, and C), indicating a fundamentally healthy market.
  • Apartment Rent Growth Trends: Seattle’s rent growth is being driven by the CBD’s 4.7% annual increase, compared to 1.6% in suburban areas, as Class A assets surge 4.3% while Class B/C trail at 2.5%, underscoring a clear flight-to-quality and renewed downtown momentum.
  • Downtown Leads Recovery: Downtown Seattle is the top performer with 5.8% rent growth, signaling a strong return-to-office trend and renewed urban demand.

Summary: Seattle's market has achieved an ideal equilibrium, de-risking investments. The 60% drop in construction is a powerful catalyst for future rent growth. The downtown recovery points to specific submarket opportunities for outsized returns.

Seattle Market: Actionable Conclusion

The Seattle multifamily market has successfully transitioned from oversupplied to a state of healthy equilibrium, presenting a stable investment landscape. Despite a soft job market, strong underlying demand and a collapsed construction pipeline have stabilized vacancy at an impressive 4.0%. This supply-constrained outlook provides a clear runway for renewed rent growth and value appreciation. The robust recovery in Downtown Seattle signals that now is the time to focus on urban assets poised to capture return-to-office momentum. For current owners, the market supports a "hold and grow" strategy, focusing on pushing rents as fundamentals tighten. For buyers, the balanced risk profile and premium long-term outlook justify Seattle's higher price point, making it a prime target for capital preservation and steady growth.

Capital Markets & Investment Outlook

Transaction Freeze Thaws, But Spreads Remain Tight

  • National apartment transaction volume has returned to pre-pandemic norms, indicating the market is functioning again.
  • The Pacific Northwest is lagging this recovery, with Seattle down 14% and Portland down 6% from pre-pandemic transaction levels.
  • Seattle commands a premium price ($262K/unit) and lower cap rate (5.3%), while Portland aligns with the national average ($201K/unit, 5.9% cap rate).
  • The spread between apartment cap rates and the 10-year Treasury is historically narrow, posing a risk to values if Treasury rates rise or spreads revert to the mean.

Summary: The national transaction market has normalized, but a buyer-seller pricing gap persists in the PNW, particularly Seattle. The tight cap rate spread is a key risk to monitor; however, expected Fed cuts should provide a favorable interest rate environment that mitigates some of this risk for new acquisitions.
